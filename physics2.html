18. Conservation of Energy

Energy Stores and Transfers

Energy can be stored in different ways:

  • Thermal energy - energy stored in the temperature of an object
  • Kinetic energy - energy stored in moving objects: \( E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
  • Gravitational potential energy - energy stored in raised objects: \( E_p = mgh \)
  • Elastic potential energy - energy stored in stretched springs: \( E_e = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \)
  • Chemical energy - energy stored in chemical bonds
  • Nuclear energy - energy stored in atomic nuclei
  • Magnetic energy - energy stored in magnetic fields
  • Electrostatic energy - energy stored in electric fields

Work Done

When energy is transferred mechanically (by a force moving through a distance):

\[ W = F \times d \]

Where:

  • \( W \) = work done (J)
  • \( F \) = force (N)
  • \( d \) = distance moved in direction of force (m)

Energy Transfer Diagrams

Fig 1.1: Sankey diagram showing energy transfers in a light bulb

Key points about energy transfer diagrams:

  • The width of arrows represents the amount of energy
  • Useful energy output is typically shown going right
  • Wasted energy (often as heat) is shown diverging from the main arrow

Efficiency

Efficiency measures how much useful energy is transferred compared to the total energy input:

\[ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Useful energy output}}{\text{Total energy input}} \times 100\% \]

Or alternatively:

\[ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Useful power output}}{\text{Total power input}} \times 100\% \]

Example Calculation

A motor lifts a 50kg mass 10m vertically in 5 seconds. The electrical power input is 1.2kW. Calculate the efficiency.

Solution:

  1. Calculate useful energy output (GPE gained): \[ E_p = mgh = 50 \times 9.8 \times 10 = 4900\,\text{J} \]
  2. Calculate useful power output: \[ P_{\text{useful}} = \frac{4900}{5} = 980\,\text{W} \]
  3. Calculate efficiency: \[ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{980}{1200} \times 100\% = 81.7\% \]

Specific Heat Capacity

The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1kg of a substance by 1°C:

\[ \Delta E = mc\Delta\theta \]

Where:

  • \( \Delta E \) = change in thermal energy (J)
  • \( m \) = mass (kg)
  • \( c \) = specific heat capacity (J/kg°C)
  • \( \Delta\theta \) = temperature change (°C)

Power

The rate of energy transfer or work done:

\[ P = \frac{E}{t} \]

Where:

  • \( P \) = power (W)
  • \( E \) = energy transferred (J)
  • \( t \) = time (s)

19. Waves and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

Wave Properties

Fig 2.1: Transverse wave diagram showing key properties

Key wave properties:

  • Amplitude (A) - maximum displacement from equilibrium (m)
  • Wavelength (λ) - distance between identical points on consecutive waves (m)
  • Frequency (f) - number of waves passing a point per second (Hz)
  • Period (T) - time for one complete wave cycle (s): \( T = \frac{1}{f} \)
  • Wave speed (v) - speed at which energy is transferred (m/s): \( v = f\lambda \)

Wave Equation

\[ v = f\lambda \]

Where:

  • \( v \) = wave speed (m/s)
  • \( f \) = frequency (Hz)
  • \( \lambda \) = wavelength (m)

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

Transverse Waves

  • Oscillations perpendicular to direction of energy transfer
  • Examples: electromagnetic waves, water waves, S-waves (seismic)
  • Can be polarized

Longitudinal Waves

  • Oscillations parallel to direction of energy transfer
  • Examples: sound waves, P-waves (seismic)
  • Cannot be polarized
  • Show areas of compression and rarefaction
EM Wave Applications and Dangers
Type Wavelength Range Uses Hazards

Required Practical: Measuring Wave Speed

Method (Ripple Tank):

  1. Set up ripple tank with vibrating dipper
  2. Measure distance between 10 wave crests
  3. Calculate wavelength: \( \lambda = \frac{\text{distance}}{10} \)
  4. Measure frequency using strobe light
  5. Calculate speed: \( v = f\lambda \)

Method (String Waves):

  1. Attach string to vibration generator
  2. Adjust frequency until standing wave forms
  3. Measure distance between nodes for \( \lambda \)
  4. Use \( v = f\lambda \)

Variables:

  • Independent: Frequency of dipper (Hz)
  • Dependent: Wavelength (λ) measured
  • Control:
    • Water depth (keep at 1cm)
    • Temperature of water (affects viscosity)
    • Same light source for strobe

Improving Reliability:

  1. Repeat measurements: Measure 10 wavelengths 3 times, take average.
  2. Calibrate strobe: Use known frequency (e.g., 50Hz) to verify.
  3. Reduce parallax error: View waves at eye level when measuring.

    • 21. Forces and Energy

    Work Done and Energy Transfer

    \[ W = F \times d \]

    Where:

    • \( W \) = work done (J)
    • \( F \) = force in direction of motion (N)
    • \( d \) = distance moved (m)

    Example Calculation

    A 50N force pushes a box 3m across a floor. Calculate work done.

    Solution:

    \[ W = 50 \times 3 = 150\,\text{J} \]

    Energy Types and Formulas

    Kinetic Energy (KE)

    \[ KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

    Where:

    • \( m \) = mass (kg)
    • \( v \) = velocity (m/s)

    Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)

    \[ GPE = mgh \]

    Where:

    • \( h \) = height (m)
    • \( g \) = 9.81 m/s²

    Elastic Potential Energy (EPE)

    \[ EPE = \frac{1}{2}ke^2 \]

    Where:

    • \( k \) = spring constant (N/m)
    • \( e \) = extension (m)

    Required Practical: Hooke's Law & Elastic Energy

    Equipment:

    • Spring
    • Clamp stand
    • Masses (100g increments)
    • Ruler
    • Pointer (optional)

    Method:

    1. Suspend spring from clamp stand.
    2. Measure original length with no load.
    3. Add 100g mass, record new length.
    4. Calculate extension: \( e = \text{new length} - \text{original length} \).
    5. Repeat up to 500g.
    6. Plot force (\( F = mg \)) vs. extension graph.
    7. Determine spring constant \( k \) from gradient (\( F = ke \)).
    Safety: Wear goggles in case spring snaps.

    Variables:

    • Independent: Mass added (kg)
    • Dependent: Extension (e) measured
    • Control:
      • Same spring (don't change mid-experiment)
      • Temperature (spring constant varies with T)
      • Initial length (measure from same reference point)

    Improving Reliability:

    1. Pre-stretch spring: Add/remove 100g mass 3 times before recording data to eliminate hysteresis.
    2. Use pointer: Attach lightweight pointer to spring for precise length measurements.
    3. Check elastic limit: Ensure spring returns to original length after unloading.

    Power

    \[ P = \frac{W}{t} \]

    Where:

    • \( P \) = power (W)
    • \( W \) = work done (J)
    • \( t \) = time (s)

    Example Calculation

    A crane lifts a 100kg mass 20m in 10s. Calculate power output.

    Solution:

    1. Calculate work done (GPE gained): \[ W = mgh = 100 \times 9.81 \times 20 = 19,620\,\text{J} \]
    2. Calculate power: \[ P = \frac{19,620}{10} = 1,962\,\text{W} \]

    22. Electricity and Circuits

    Current, PD, and Resistance

    \[ V = IR \]

    Where:

    • \( V \) = potential difference (V)
    • \( I \) = current (A)
    • \( R \) = resistance (Ω)

    Series and Parallel Circuits

    Series Circuits

    • Current same everywhere
    • Total resistance: \[ R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + \ldots \]
    • Voltage splits across components

    Parallel Circuits

    • Voltage same across branches
    • Total resistance: \[ \frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \ldots \]
    • Current splits across branches

    Required Practical: IV Characteristics

    Equipment:

    • Power supply (0-12V DC)
    • Ammeter
    • Voltmeter
    • Resistor/filament bulb/diode
    • Rheostat (variable resistor)
    • Connecting wires

    Method:

    1. Set up series circuit with component, ammeter, and power supply.
    2. Connect voltmeter in parallel across component.
    3. Vary voltage from 0V in 0.5V increments using rheostat.
    4. Record current \( I \) and voltage \( V \) at each step.
    5. Reverse power supply connections for diode (if testing reverse bias).
    6. Plot \( I \) (y-axis) vs. \( V \) (x-axis) graph.
    Expected Results:
    • Resistor: Straight line through origin (Ohmic conductor)
    • Filament bulb: Curve flattening as \( V \) increases
    • Diode: Current only flows above ~0.6V (forward bias)
    Safety: Disconnect circuit when adjusting wires.

    Variables:

    • Independent: Voltage (V)
    • Dependent: Current (I) measured
    • Control:
      • Same component (don't swap resistors/bulbs)
      • Room temperature (affects resistance)
      • Wire thickness/length

    Improving Reliability:

    1. Wait for stabilization: Allow bulb/diode to reach steady temperature at each voltage.
    2. Reverse polarity checks: For diodes, test both forward and reverse bias.
    3. Use digital meters: Analog meters introduce parallax errors.

    Energy and Power in Circuits

    \[ P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \]

    Where:

    • \( P \) = power (W)
    • Energy transferred: \[ E = Pt = IVt \]

    24. Matter

    Density

    \[ \rho = \frac{m}{V} \]

    Where:

    • \( \rho \) = density (kg/m³)
    • \( m \) = mass (kg)
    • \( V \) = volume (m³)

    Required Practical: Measuring Density

    Regular solid:

    1. Measure dimensions with ruler
    2. Calculate volume \( V = l \times w \times h \)
    3. Weigh object to find mass \( m \)
    4. Calculate \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \)

    Irregular solid:

    1. Fill eureka can to spout level
    2. Submerge object, collect displaced water
    3. Measure volume of displaced water
    4. Weigh object for mass
    5. Calculate density

    Specific Heat Capacity

    \[ E = mc\Delta\theta \]

    Where:

    • \( E \) = energy (J)
    • \( c \) = SHC (J/kg°C)
    • \( \Delta\theta \) = temp change (°C)

    Required Practical: Measuring SHC

    1. Measure mass \( m \) of block
    2. Insert heater and thermometer
    3. Record initial temperature
    4. Supply known energy \( E = VIt \)
    5. Record temperature rise \( \Delta\theta \)
    6. Calculate \( c = \frac{E}{m\Delta\theta} \)

    Specific Latent Heat

    \[ E = mL \]

    Where:

    • \( L \) = specific latent heat (J/kg)
    • \( L_f \) = fusion (melting/freezing)
    • \( L_v \) = vaporization (boiling/condensing)

    Required Practical: Specific Heat Capacity

    Equipment:

    • Metal block with holes for heater/thermometer
    • Joulemeter or ammeter + voltmeter
    • Power supply
    • Thermometer
    • Stopwatch

    Method:

    1. Measure mass \( m \) of block.
    2. Record initial temperature \( \theta_1 \).
    3. Supply known energy \( E = VIt \) for 10 minutes.
    4. Record final temperature \( \theta_2 \).
    5. Calculate SHC: \( c = \frac{E}{m(\theta_2 - \theta_1)} \).
    6. Repeat with insulation to reduce heat loss.
    Safety: Allow block to cool between trials.

    Required Practical: Determining Density

    Part A: Regular Solid (Cube)

    Equipment:
    • Metal cube
    • Vernier calipers/ruler
    • Digital balance
    Method:
    1. Measure mass \( m \) of cube using balance.
    2. Measure side length \( l \) at 3 positions.
    3. Calculate volume: \( V = l^3 \).
    4. Calculate density: \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \).

    Part B: Irregular Solid (Marble)

    Equipment:
    • Eureka can
    • Measuring cylinder
    • Marble
    • Digital balance
    Method:
    1. Measure mass \( m \) of marble.
    2. Fill eureka can until water flows from spout.
    3. Submerge marble completely, collect displaced water.
    4. Measure volume \( V \) of displaced water.
    5. Calculate density: \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \).
    Safety: Dry marbles before mass measurement.

    Variables:

    • Independent: Object type (cube vs marble)
    • Dependent: Density (ρ) calculated
    • Control:
      • Measurement tools (same ruler/balance for all)
      • Temperature (affects water density in displacement)
      • Dry objects before mass measurement

    Improving Reliability:

    1. Measure multiple cubes/marbles: 3 of each to identify anomalies.
    2. Zero instruments: Calibrate balance and check ruler starts at 0.
    3. Meniscus correction: Read water level at eye level in measuring cylinder.
